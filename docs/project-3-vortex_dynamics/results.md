## 1. Initial Stage (t ≈ 0)

### Observation

- Smooth horizontal velocity profile  
- No visible instability  

### Explanation

- Flow is stable initially  
- No disturbances have grown yet  

---

## 2. Linear Growth Stage

### Observation

- Small waves appear at the interface  
- Slight bending of shear layer  

### Explanation

- Initial perturbation starts growing  
- This is the **linear instability phase**  

---

## 3. Nonlinear Growth (Vortex Formation)

### Observation

- Waves roll into spiral structures  
- Clear vortex formation  

### Explanation

- Shear instability amplifies disturbances  
- Flow transitions from smooth → rotational  

This is the key stage of **Kelvin–Helmholtz instability**

---

## 4. Fully Developed Vortices

### Observation

- Large vortex structures form  
- Multiple rotating regions visible  

### Explanation

- Instability becomes nonlinear  
- Energy transfers into rotational motion  

---

## 5. Vortex Interaction

### Observation

- Vortices stretch and interact  
- Structures become more complex  

### Explanation

- Vortex interaction leads to mixing  
- Flow becomes more chaotic  

---

## Key Insights

- Velocity difference drives instability  
- Small disturbances grow rapidly  
- Vortices form due to shear  
- Lower viscosity → sharper vortices  

---

## Effect of Parameters

### 1. Shear Thickness

- Smaller thickness → faster instability  
- Larger thickness → slower growth  

---

### 2. Viscosity

- Low viscosity → sharp vortex structures  
- High viscosity → smooth flow  

---

### 3. Grid Resolution

- Higher resolution → better vortex detail  
- Lower resolution → numerical diffusion  

---

## Final Understanding

The simulation shows how a simple velocity difference can lead to complex vortex structures through instability.

---

## One-Line Summary

A small disturbance in a shear flow grows over time and rolls into vortices due to Kelvin–Helmholtz instability.
