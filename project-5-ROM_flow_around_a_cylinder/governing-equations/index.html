    Governing Equations

    Introduction

    The flow around a circular cylinder at Reynolds number Re = 100 is governed by the incompressible Navier–Stokes equations.

    Since the objective of this project is to perform Reduced Order Modeling (ROM) using Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), it is important to first understand the full-order mathematical model (FOM) from which the reduced model is derived.

    In ROM, we do not directly solve the reduced equations first.

    Instead, we follow:

    Full CFD Simulation → Snapshot Generation → POD → Reduced Order Representation

    This means the governing equations of the CFD simulation form the mathematical foundation of the entire ROM framework.

    Physical Assumptions

    The simulation is based on the following assumptions:

    • Two-dimensional flow
    • Incompressible fluid
    • Laminar flow regime
    • Newtonian fluid
    • Constant density
    • Constant viscosity
    • Isothermal flow
    • No body forces
    • No heat transfer effects

    Since the Reynolds number is:

                Re = 100

    the flow lies in the laminar unsteady regime where wake instability and vortex shedding begin to appear.

    This makes it an ideal benchmark problem for POD-based Reduced Order Modeling.

    Continuity Equation (Mass Conservation)

    For incompressible flow, conservation of mass requires:

            ∇ · u = 0

    where:

            u = (ux, uy)

    is the velocity vector.

    This equation ensures that the fluid volume remains conserved and no artificial compression occurs inside the computational domain.

    Physically, this means:

    Whatever fluid enters a control volume must also leave it.

    This is the first condition that must always be satisfied in incompressible CFD.

    Momentum Equation (Navier–Stokes Equation)

    The incompressible Navier–Stokes momentum equation is:

        ∂u/∂t + (u · ∇)u = -∇p + ν∇²u

    where:

        - u = velocity vector
    - p = pressure
    - ν = kinematic viscosity
    - t = time

    This equation represents Newton’s Second Law applied to fluid motion.

    It balances:

        - acceleration
    - convection
    - pressure forces
    - viscous diffusion

    and governs the entire flow physics around the cylinder.

    Physical Meaning of Each Term

    1. Transient Term

    ∂u/∂t

    This represents:

    Change of velocity with time

    This term is responsible for capturing:

        - unsteady flow behavior
    - wake development
    - vortex shedding
    - time-dependent flow structures

    Without this term, the simulation would only capture steady-state flow.

    Since cylinder wake flow is inherently unsteady, this term is essential.

    2. Convective Term

        (u · ∇)u

    This represents:

    Momentum carried by the moving fluid itself

    This term is responsible for:

        - flow separation
    - wake formation
    - nonlinear flow behavior
    - vortex transport

    This is the most important source of complexity in bluff-body external aerodynamics.

    It is also the reason why vortex shedding becomes difficult to predict and why ROM becomes useful.

    3. Pressure Gradient Term

    -∇p

    This represents:

    Force generated by pressure differences

    Pressure redistributes momentum throughout the domain and helps enforce incompressibility.

    In Basilisk, pressure is solved using the pressure Poisson equation during pressure–velocity coupling.

    This was also the main source of convergence warnings during the project.

    4. Viscous Diffusion Term

    ν∇²u

    This represents:

    Momentum diffusion caused by viscosity

    It smooths the velocity field and resists strong velocity gradients.

    At Re = 100, viscosity is still significant and strongly affects:

    - wake stability
- boundary layer growth
- vortex formation

    Reynolds Number

    The most important dimensionless parameter for this problem is the Reynolds number:

    Re = UD/ν

    where:

    - U = free-stream inlet velocity
- D = cylinder diameter
- ν = kinematic viscosity

    In this project:

    Re = 100

    This Reynolds number determines the competition between:

    - inertial forces
- viscous forces

    At this value:

    - the wake becomes unstable
- recirculation appears
- periodic vortex shedding begins
- transient behavior becomes important

    This is exactly why POD becomes meaningful.

    If Re were too low, the flow would remain mostly steady and ROM would show only one dominant steady mode.

    Boundary Conditions

    Correct boundary conditions are critical for capturing realistic wake behavior.

    1. Inlet Boundary

    Uniform free-stream velocity is imposed:

    ux = U0

uy = 0

    This creates the incoming flow approaching the cylinder.

    It represents an external flow problem rather than an internal confined flow like the lid-driven cavity.

    2. Cylinder Surface

    No-slip wall condition:

    ux = 0

uy = 0

    This creates:

    • boundary layer development
    • adverse pressure gradient
    • flow separation
    • wake formation

    This is where the most important flow physics originate.

    Without no-slip walls, there would be no vortex shedding.

    3. Outlet Boundary

    Outflow boundary condition:

    ∂u/∂n = 0

    This allows the fluid to leave the computational domain naturally without artificial reflection.

    It helps preserve realistic wake development behind the cylinder.

    4. Top and Bottom Boundaries

    Free-slip / open boundaries are used to avoid artificial confinement effects.

    This allows the wake to develop naturally without strong wall interference.

    Embedded Boundary Method in Basilisk

    Instead of generating a body-fitted mesh around the circular cylinder, Basilisk uses:

        "embed.h"

    which applies the Embedded Boundary Method.

    The cylinder geometry is defined using:

    solid(cs, fs, sq(x) + sq(y) - sq(RADIUS));

    where:

    - cs = cell fraction
- fs = face fraction

    This method allows:

    - easy geometry representation
- Cartesian adaptive grids
- simpler meshing
- faster solver implementation
- robust boundary handling

    This is one of the strongest advantages of Basilisk over traditional mesh-heavy CFD workflows.

    Connection to Reduced Order Modeling (ROM)

    The Navier–Stokes equations define the Full Order Model (FOM) which is computationally expensive. A full CFD simulation may contain Thousands to millions of degrees of freedom and repeated solving becomes expensive. POD is applied to the CFD solution to build the:

    Reduced Order Model (ROM), Instead of solving the full system repeatedly, ROM represents the flow using Few dominant spatial modes + temporal coefficients. This dramatically reduces computational cost while preserving the dominant system physics. This is the core purpose of this project.

    POD Mathematical Idea

    The CFD snapshots are arranged into a matrix:

    X = [x1  x2  x3  ...  xn]

    where each column is one velocity snapshot.

    Then Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is applied:

    X = UΣVᵀ

    where:

    - U = spatial POD modes
- Σ = singular values (energy content)
- Vᵀ = temporal coefficients

    This allows the flow to be written as:

    Flow ≈ Mean + Few Dominant Modes

    instead of solving the full Navier–Stokes equations every time.

    This is how CFD becomes computationally efficient.

    Why POD is Useful Here

    For cylinder flow, POD helps answer:

        What are the most energetic structures in the wake?  and
    Is the flow dominated by steady wake behavior or oscillatory vortex shedding?

    In this project, POD showed:

    - Mode 1 captured almost all system energy
- Higher modes were weak

    This indicated that Mean wake structure dominated over oscillatory vortex shedding. This is a physically meaningful engineering conclusion.

    Final Insight

    • The governing equations remain exactly the same.

    • ROM does not change the physics.

    It changes only:

    - How efficiently the physics is represented

- The Navier–Stokes equations provide the full physical truth.

- POD helps compress that truth into a smaller, faster, and more useful engineering model.

- That is the core philosophy of Reduced Order Modeling.
